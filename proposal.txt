Project Tittle
Analyzing the Employee Customer Performance Chain: A Branch-Level Study of National Choice Bank
Project Objective
To analyse and understand how employee perceptions (such as engagement, communication, training, and teamwork) influence customer satisfaction and loyalty, and how these customer outcomes ultimately impact branch-level performance at National Choice Bank (NCB).
Project content:
This study investigates the interconnected relationships among employee perceptions, customer satisfaction, and branch-level performance at National Choice Bank (NCB). Using a combined dataset of employee survey responses, customer satisfaction data, and operational performance metrics, the project explores how internal workforce factors such as engagement, communication, training, and teamwork shape customer service quality and customer loyalty. The dataset consists of over 2,200 employee responses, more than 14,000 customer evaluations, and operational indicators for 128 bank branches. Through descriptive analytics, regression modeling, cluster analysis, and interactive Shiny dashboards in R, the analysis seeks to quantify the employee–customer–performance chain central to NCB’s organizational strategy.
The findings show that employee perceptions of engagement, communication, involvement, and training are positively associated with customer service quality. Customer service quality strongly predicts customer loyalty, which in turn contributes to differences in productivity across branches. Metropolitan branches tend to outperform non-metropolitan branches, likely due to differences in customer traffic and operational scale. Cluster analysis further reveals distinct branch profiles, ranging from high-engagement/high-performance clusters to low-engagement/low-performance ones. The results reinforce research in organizational behavior emphasizing the importance of workforce management in shaping customer outcomes and
business performance. The project concludes with recommendations for improving employee engagement, service quality, and branch-level productivity through data-driven management strategies.

Metric
Description / Reason
Mean Absolute Error (MAE)
Expected value of absolute error (L1 loss); intuitive and less sensitive to outliers.
Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE)
Square root of mean squared error (MSE), which measures squared errors; penalises large errors more heavily.
R² (Coefficient of Determination)
Proportion of variance explained by the model; useful for comparing models.
Metric
Description / Reason
Accuracy
Proportion of correct classifications; simple metric but can be misleading for imbalanced data.
Precision
Proportion of positive predictions that are correct; measures false positives.
Recall (True Positive Rate)
Proportion of actual positives correctly identified; measures false negatives.
F1 Score
Harmonic mean of precision and recall; balances precision and recall for imbalanced datasets.
ROC–AUC
Area under the ROC curve; probability that the model ranks a random positive sample higher than a negative.
Metric
Description / Reason
Silhouette Score
Measures how similar a sample is to its own cluster compared to other clusters; values close to 1 indicate well-separated clusters.
Davies–Bouldin Index
Average ratio of within-cluster scatter to between-cluster separation; lower values indicate better clustering.
Validation Strategy
1.
Initial Train/Test Split:
•
Hold out a test set (e.g., 20% of branches) to obtain an unbiased estimate of the final model’s performance. Holding out data helps prevent over fitting and ensures the model generalizes.
•
Optionally create a validation set (e.g., 10–15%) for hyper parameter tuning. Common split ratios include 70/15/15 or 80/10/10.
2.
K Fold Cross Validation on the Training Set:
•
Divide the training data into k folds (commonly k=5 or 10). Train the model on k–1 folds and validate on the remaining fold, repeating the process k times. Average the performance across folds for a more reliable estimate.
•
Cross validation mitigates the randomness of a single split and makes efficient use of limited data. It is especially beneficial for smaller datasets such as the 85 or 128 branches.
3.
Stratified CV for Classification:
•
When predicting categorical outcomes (e.g., high vs. low customer loyalty), use stratified cross validation to preserve class proportions within each fold.
4.
Nested Cross Validation for Hyper Parameter Tuning:
•
If the model requires hyper parameter tuning (e.g., regularization strength), implement nested CV: an inner loop for parameter tuning and an outer loop for performance estimation, to avoid leaking information from the test fold.
